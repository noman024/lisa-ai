# Life Insurance Support Knowledge Base

## Term Life Insurance

**Definition:** Term life insurance provides a death benefit to beneficiaries if the insured person dies during a specified term, such as 10, 20, or 30 years. It does not build cash value; coverage ends when the term expires unless the policy is renewed or converted per policy terms.

**Benefits:** Simple structure, often the lowest initial premium for a given death benefit, level premium options for the duration of the term, and a clear death benefit for income replacement or debt protection. Many policies allow optional riders for accelerated death benefit or waiver of premium.

**Eligibility:** Insurers consider age, health history, medical exam or accelerated underwriting, occupation, and hazardous activities. Younger, healthier applicants typically receive better rates. Maximum issue ages and minimum coverage amounts vary by carrier and product. Tobacco use, height and weight, and family medical history affect underwriting.

**Claims:** The beneficiary or representative notifies the insurance company, completes claim forms, and provides a certified death certificate and any required proof of insurable interest. The carrier reviews the claim, validates the policy is in force, and pays the death benefit to named beneficiaries, subject to policy exclusions such as contestability period rules.

## Whole Life Insurance

**Definition:** Whole life insurance is permanent life insurance with a level premium and a death benefit that lasts for the insured's lifetime, as long as required premiums are paid. It includes a cash value component that grows with guaranteed and sometimes non-guaranteed elements depending on the product design.

**Benefits:** Lifetime coverage, fixed premiums in many traditional designs, cash value that may be borrowed against or withdrawn subject to terms, and potential dividends on participating policies (not guaranteed). Can support estate planning and final expense needs.

**Eligibility:** Similar underwriting to other permanent products: age, health, lifestyle, and financial justification for the face amount. Underwriting is often stricter for large face amounts, which may require financial documentation.

**Claims:** The claim process is like term life: death certificate, claim forms, and beneficiary verification. Cash value and loan balances may reduce the net death benefit if loans are outstanding. The insurer issues payment after review.

## Universal Life Insurance

**Definition:** Universal life (UL) is permanent insurance with flexible premiums and an adjustable death benefit within policy limits, linked to a cash value account that earns interest or is tied to a crediting method specified in the contract (e.g., current interest, indexed, or variable subaccounts for variable UL).

**Benefits:** Flexibility in premium timing and amount within limits, potential for cash value growth, and the ability to adjust the death benefit in some cases. Policy charges and cost of insurance are deducted from the account value; insufficient funding can cause the policy to lapse if not managed.

**Eligibility:** Health and age underwriting apply. Large policies may need financial underwriting. Producers and carriers must follow suitability and disclosure rules. Indexed and variable products may have additional eligibility and product-specific disclosures.

**Claims:** Beneficiaries file a death claim with documentation as with other life products. Any loans or withdrawals can affect the benefit paid. Lapsed policies may not pay a benefit; always verify the policy was in good standing at death.

## General Benefits of Life Insurance

**Income replacement:** Provides funds for dependents if the primary earner dies. **Debt coverage:** Can pay mortgages, student loans, or business obligations. **Final expenses:** Can cover funeral and end-of-life costs. **Estate liquidity:** Can provide cash for taxes or bequests. **Business uses:** Key person coverage, buy-sell funding, and executive benefits. **Charitable giving:** Can name a charity as beneficiary.

Riders may add long-term care-style benefits, disability waiver, or child term coverage where available. Actual riders depend on the carrier and state approval.

---

## Eligibility and Underwriting (Cross-Cutting)

**Age:** Products have minimum and maximum issue ages. **Health:** Underwriting class (preferred plus, preferred, standard, table ratings) depends on medical history, labs, and build. **Lifestyle:** Aviation, diving, and hazardous avocations may be rated or excluded. **Residency and travel:** Some risks are declined or limited. **Financial:** Large amounts require justification of insurable interest and need. **Legal capacity:** The applicant must be able to enter into a contract. **Fraud and misrepresentation:** Material misstatements can void coverage or affect claims in the contestability period, typically the first two years in many U.S. jurisdictions, subject to state law and policy terms.

Group life through employers may have simplified or guaranteed issue during enrollment, with different rules.

## Claims Process (General Steps)

1. **Notify the insurer** as soon as possible after the insured's death.  
2. **Obtain claim forms** from the carrier or agent.  
3. **Submit required documents:** certified death certificate, claim form, and identification. Some cases need medical records, police reports, or foreign death paperwork.  
4. **Underwriting and investigation:** The company verifies policy status, cause of death, and exclusions (e.g., suicide clause during initial policy period, where applicable by law and contract).  
5. **Payout options:** Lump sum, retained asset account, annuity settlement, or other options per policy.  
6. **Timing:** Varies by jurisdiction and complexity; straightforward claims may be paid in a few weeks.  

Contestability, homicide investigations, and incomplete documentation can delay payment. Policy loans reduce the net benefit.

## Comparison: Term vs Whole Life (Summary)

| Aspect | Term Life | Whole Life |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| Duration | Specified years | Lifetime (if maintained) |
| Cash value | No | Yes |
| Premium | Lower initially for same death benefit in many cases | Higher; level in traditional designs |
| Use cases | Temporary needs: mortgage, children through college | Permanent needs, cash value, estate liquidity |

**Definition:** Use term for time-bound, high-amount protection. Use whole life when lifetime coverage and cash value are aligned with the client's goals and budget. Always compare illustrations and read the full policy contract; this table is a general summary only and not a recommendation.

**Eligibility and pricing differ** by product; what is affordable in term may not be the same face amount in whole life.  
**Claims** follow the same family of steps (documentation, verification, payment) for both, subject to the specific policy’s terms.
